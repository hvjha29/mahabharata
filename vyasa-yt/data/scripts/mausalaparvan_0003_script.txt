In the previous chapter, we witnessed the ominous signs that foretold the impending doom of the Vrishnis and Andhakas, the clans of Krishna and his kin.

As the days turned into weeks, the Vrishnis and Andhakas continued their lives, unaware of the dark fate that loomed over them. Time, that relentless force, seemed to weave its way through every corner of their homes, leaving behind a trail of unsettling portents.

Strange and terrifying phenomena began to manifest. The air grew heavy with the presence of great winds, which howled through the streets, heralding the destruction that was to come. Rats, once small and insignificant, grew to alarming sizes, and precious gems lay scattered across the streets, unclaimed and forgotten. Parrots, usually cheerful and melodious, cried out in a cacophony of "chichi-kuki," their voices never ceasing, day or night.

Even the natural world seemed to turn against them. Cranes mimicked the eerie calls of owls, and goats echoed the haunting cries of jackals. Birds with white feathers and red feet roamed the houses of the Vrishnis and Andhakas, as if driven by some unseen force. The animals, too, bore the marks of this strange transformation: from cows were born donkeys, from mares came more donkeys, and even the offspring of dogs and rats defied the natural order.

Despite these signs, the Vrishnis and Andhakas continued to commit sins without a hint of shame. They grew angry with the Brahmanas, the ancestors, and the gods, yet they still showed respect to their elders and to the revered figures of Rama and Janardana. Families began to fracture, with wives separating from their husbands and husbands from their wives.

The very elements of nature seemed to rebel. Fires burned with an unnatural vigor, their flames twisting to the left and casting separate rays of blue, red, and orange. The sun, at both its rise and set, was surrounded by deformed figures, and when food was prepared in the great kitchens, worms appeared in the dishes.

The auspicious days, once celebrated with joy, now brought only dread. As the great souls recited their prayers, their voices seemed to run ahead, but no one could be seen. The stars, too, were struck by the planets, and yet, the Vrishnis and Andhakas remained blind to their own plight.

In the heart of their abode, the conch shells sounded, and donkeys brayed with terrifying noises, filling the air with a sense of impending doom. Hrishikesha, the lord of the senses, observed these signs and, on the thirteenth day, as the new moon rose, he spoke with a heavy heart.

"Time has come full circle," he declared. "Thirty-six years have passed since the great battle of Bharata, and the omens I see now are the same as those that presaged the fall of Duryodhana and his kin. The fourteenth and fifteenth days of this month will bring about our destruction."

Gandhari, still tormented by the loss of her son and her kinsmen, had foreseen this tragedy in her grief. Her words of warning, spoken in distress, now rang true. Yudhishthira, recalling the terrible omens he had witnessed before the battle, understood the gravity of the situation.

"Long ago, when the armies were arrayed, I saw similar signs," he said. "We must act now to fulfill the truth of these omens."

Vasudeva, determined to avert the worst, commanded the warriors to undertake a pilgrimage to the ocean. "O bulls among men, you must go on a pilgrimage to the sea," he proclaimed, hoping that this act of penance might yet save them from the coming calamity.

This chapter serves as a poignant reminder of the Mahābhārata’s overarching theme: that even the most powerful and noble can be undone by the inexorable march of time and the weight of their own actions. It sets the stage for the tragic end of the Vrishni and Andhaka clans, a fall that echoes the larger cycle of rise and fall that defines the epic.
