In the previous chapter, the Pandavas, having reclaimed their kingdom and ruled with wisdom and justice, found themselves grappling with the weight of their past battles and the inevitable march of time.

As the thirty-sixth year of their reign dawned, King Yudhishthira, the eldest of the Pandavas, began to notice a series of ominous signs that troubled his heart. The winds howled with a fierce, unsettling roar, carrying sand that stung the skin. Birds flew in strange patterns, circling to the left, and the once-clear skies were now shrouded in a thick, oppressive mist. Rivers, once pristine, were now cloaked in fog, and the directions seemed to blur, as if the very fabric of the world was unraveling. Meteors rained down like burning coals, and the sun, usually a beacon of light, appeared dull and lifeless, its rays obscured by a veil of dust.

These signs, O king, were not mere coincidences but harbingers of a greater calamity. Yudhishthira, ever the thoughtful and just ruler, could not ignore these portents. His heart heavy with foreboding, he confided in his brothers, asking, "What shall we do?" For even the wisest of kings can feel the weight of destiny pressing down upon them.

Not long after, news reached the Pandavas of a devastating event that had befallen their allies, the Vrishnis. In the city of Dvaraka, a great quarrel had erupted among the Vrishnis, leading to a brutal and senseless massacre. The mighty heroes, driven by an unseen force, turned their weapons against one another, striking with maces and clubs until the streets ran red with blood. Among the fallen were the great warriors of the Vrishni and Andhaka clans, including the valiant Bhoja maharathas. Even the revered Vasudeva and Rama, though spared, could not prevent the carnage.

The news of this tragedy struck the Pandavas with a profound sense of loss and disbelief. They had long considered the Vrishnis as kin, and the thought of their destruction was like the drying up of a vast ocean. Yudhishthira, in particular, struggled to comprehend how such noble warriors could meet such a grim fate. The brothers gathered in silence, their spirits heavy with grief and their resolve shaken.

But the question remained: How did this catastrophe unfold? To understand the roots of this tragedy, we must look back to a fateful encounter in Dvaraka. The Vrishnis, known for their valor and honor, had welcomed three great sages—Vishvamitra, Kanva, and Narada—to their city. These sages, renowned for their spiritual prowess, were greeted with respect and reverence. However, a mischievous act by the young prince Sambha, son of Vasudeva, set in motion a chain of events that would lead to the clan's downfall.

Sambha, in a moment of youthful folly, dressed himself as a woman and approached the sages, claiming to be the wife of a man named Babhru, who desired a son. The sages, deceived by this ruse, unwittingly blessed Sambha, predicting that he would give birth to a terrible iron mace—a weapon that would bring about the destruction of the Vrishnis and Andhakas. Realizing their mistake, the sages, their eyes red with anger, cursed the Vrishnis, declaring that this mace would bring ruin upon them.

When the sages' curse was revealed, the Vrishni leaders, including Vasudeva, were deeply troubled. They took swift action, grinding the iron mace into fine powder and casting it into the ocean, hoping to avert the impending disaster. They also issued a strict decree: no one in the city was to consume liquor, for fear that it might inflame passions and lead to further violence. Despite these efforts, the curse could not be undone, and the seeds of destruction had already been sown.

This chapter is crucial to the larger story of the Mahābhārata because it underscores the theme of destiny and the inevitability of karma. Even the most noble and powerful cannot escape the consequences of their actions, and the tragic fate of the Vrishnis serves as a stark reminder of the cyclical nature of life and the fragility of human endeavors.
